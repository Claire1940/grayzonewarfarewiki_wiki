<Callout type="warning" title="Thermal vs. Night Vision">
While both thermal and night vision are invaluable, they serve different purposes. Thermal optics excel at detecting warm bodies, but can struggle with identifying friend or foe from a distance. Night vision provides a clearer, albeit monochromatic, view of the environment. Ideally, combine both or switch between them as the situation demands.

<Section icon={Target} title="Tactical Movement and Engagement">
Executing successful night operations goes beyond just having the right gear; it demands a refined approach to movement, engagement, and communication.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Slow and Controlled Movement">
    Reduce your movement speed. Sprinting and quick movements create noise and make you an easy target. Utilize cover and concealment, moving from shadow to shadow. **Crouch-walking** and **prone crawling** are your best friends.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Sound Discipline">
    Every sound is amplified at night. Avoid unnecessary actions like opening inventory loudly or reloading in the open. Use suppressed weapons whenever possible. Listen intently for enemy footsteps, distant gunshots, and environmental cues.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Strategic Lighting Use">
    Weapon lights and visible lasers are powerful but come at a cost. Only activate them when actively engaging a target or clearing a room. A momentary flash can give you the advantage, but prolonged use will reveal your position.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Engagement Distances">
    Night operations often favor closer-range engagements, especially without high-end optics. Understand the effective range of your chosen gear and weapons. Avoid taking long-range shots unless you are confident in your aim and have a clear escape route.
  </Step>
  <Step num="5" title="Team Communication">
    Clear and concise communication is even more critical at night. Use precise callouts for enemy positions, movement, and potential threats. Designate a lead scout and a rear guard. Consider using non-verbal cues if available.
  </Step>
</Steps>
